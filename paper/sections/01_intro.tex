\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:01_intro}

Issue Tracking Systems provide a structured way to communicate concerns related to the software project by allowing users to submit issue reports~\cite{colavito2024leveraging}. Issues reports can be classified with labels to help with triage. Effective issue report classification (IRC) results in faster issue resolution~\cite{liao2018exploring}, and is crucial for many software maintenance tasks, such as issue prioritization~\cite{catolino2019not}, issue assignment~\cite{wu2022spatial, jeong2009improving}, and developer onboarding~\cite{santos2023tag, steinmacher2018let, aracena2025applying}. However, studies show that labels are rarely applied in issue creation~\cite{antoniol2008bug, herzig2013s, cabot2015exploring, kallis2019ticket}. As a result, project maintainers must manually classify the issues for triage, which is laborious and time-consuming~\cite{fan2017road}. 

To address this problem, numerous automated IRC methods have been proposed around supervised learning over three established issue report categories: \emph{bug}, \emph{feature}, and \emph{question}~\cite{antoniol2008bug, kallis2019ticket, izadi2022catiss, aracena2025applying, aracena2024applyinglargelanguagemodels}. Early work relied on classic machine learning (ML) techniques~\cite{antoniol2008bug, kallis2019ticket, fan2017road, herzig2013s}, while more recent studies fine-tune transformer models on labeled issue reports~\cite{izadi2022catiss, izadi2022predicting, trautsch2022predicting, bharadwaj2022github, colavito2022issue, wang2021well, gomes2023bert}. These approaches require substantial amounts of training data and often struggle with minority classes~\cite{izadi2022catiss, izadi2022predicting, colavito2022issue, heo2025study, vargovich2023givemelabeledissues}. The current state-of-the-art methods instruction-tune Large Language Models (LLMs) using Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora})~\cite{heo2025study, aracena2025applying, aracena2024applyinglargelanguagemodels}. However, fine-tuning LLMs is computationally expensive and must be repeated whenever the model is swapped or to incorporate new labeled issues that accumulate in the project~\cite{fan2017road, kallis2019ticket}.


Prior work shows that placing a few demonstration examples in the prompt can boost an LLM's performance on software-related tasks~\cite{assi2026llm, le2023log} through in-context learning~\cite{brown2020language}. Since few-shot prompting enhances performance without model training or weight updates, it has the potential to serve as a cost-efficient alternative to fine-tuning LLMs for IRC. Few-shot performance, however, depends on the choice of in-context examples: retrieval-based example selection outperforms random selection~\cite{liu2022makes}, and the most similar neighbors of a query tend to share the same label, making them informative demonstrations for classification~\cite{cover1967nearest}. The combination of these insights leads to retrieval-augmented few-shot prompting, yet a systematic evaluation of this approach for IRC remains largely underexplored in the literature.

To bridge this gap, we propose \textbf{R}etrieval-\textbf{A}ugmented \textbf{G}enerative \textbf{TAG}ging (\ragtag). Given a query issue, \ragtag\ retrieves its top-$k$ most similar labeled issues from an index of embedded historical issue reports and presents them as classified examples in the prompt. Our evaluation (\Cref{sec:ragtag}) reveals that the \emph{question} class has the weakest \ragtag\ performance, and that question issues are frequently misclassified as bugs. We hypothesize that bug-labeled neighbors retrieved for true-question queries reinforce this misclassification. To tackle this problem, we propose \textbf{B}alanced \textbf{R}etrieval-\textbf{A}ugmented \textbf{G}enerative \textbf{TAG}ging (\bragtag), a \ragtag\ variant that drops the bug-labeled neighbors whenever their count is within a small margin $m$ of the question-labeled count ($N_\text{bug} - N_\text{question} \le m$) before prompt construction.

Following the standard $k$-nearest-neighbor classification framework~\cite{cover1967nearest}, with neighbors weighted by their cosine similarity to the query~\cite{dudani1976distance}, we propose \textbf{Vo}ting-based \textbf{TAG}ging (\votag). For a given query issue, \votag\ retrieves its top-$k$ neighbors,  similar to \ragtag\ and \bragtag, and predicts the query label by a simple similarity-weighted vote on the neighbors' labels.  \votag\ plays three roles in this study: First, it establishes a retrieval-only baseline that LLM-based methods must clear to justify their cost. Second, it acts as an ablation that isolates the contribution of the LLM from that of retrieval in \ragtag and \bragtag. Finally, its performance as a function of $k$ informs the $k$-grid we evaluate for \ragtag\ and \bragtag.

We evaluate our proposed methods against the established LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} fine-tuning baseline three retrieval-based methods against the established ~\cite{} on their 11-project, 6{,}600-issue benchmark, across the Qwen2.5-Instruct family at 3B, 7B, 14B, and 32B parameters~\cite{yang2024qwen25}.

We show that retrieval-augmented few-shot is competitive with fine-tuning at a fraction of the data and compute cost. Aggregated across the four model sizes, \ragtag\ in the project-specific (PS) setting trails fine-tune in the project-agnostic (PA) setting by only $0.020$ macro $F_1$ (paired bootstrap 95\% CI $[-0.028, -0.013]$) and is statistically tied at Qwen-32B (\Cref{sec:method-comparison}). \bragtag's debiasing closes the remaining gap: the aggregate $(\bragtag - \text{fine-tune})$ difference is $+0.001$ with paired bootstrap 95\% CI $[-0.007, +0.008]$, passing TOST equivalence at $\delta{=}0.01$. \ragtag\ and \bragtag\ reach this performance using $11\times$ less labeled data per project---300 issues for retrieval vs.\ 3{,}300 for fine-tuning---and 25--47\% less peak GPU memory (47/31/25/29\% at 3B/7B/14B/32B), since no training-time activations, gradients, or optimizer state are required.

We further show that the question$\rightarrow$bug bias visible in every LLM-based method we evaluate can be reduced without retraining. At each model's best $k$, \ragtag\ misclassifies 26--36\% of true-question issues as bugs; \bragtag's margin-based filter reduces this to 19--27\% while leaving bug and feature $F_1$ essentially unchanged (\Cref{sec:bragtag}). Even without any LLM at inference, retrieval alone is closer to a billion-parameter model than one might expect: \votag\ reaches macro $F_1 = 0.595$ at $k{=}15$ in the PS setting, within $0.018$ of Qwen-3B's zero-shot $0.613$ (\Cref{sec:rq1,sec:ragtag}). A manual analysis of Qwen-32B errors traces 77\% of remaining misclassifications to users filing issues under the wrong issue template rather than to LLM reasoning failures, pointing to where future IRC effort is most likely to pay off (\Cref{sec:disc-failures}).
